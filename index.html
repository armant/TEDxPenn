    TEDxPenn2015: What Lies Ahead



    How we perceive the past and the present affects our imagination and expectation of what lies ahead. Our beliefs and decisions shape the future. In this age, we are facing some of the greatest challenges in mankind’s history, such as climate change, poverty, oppression, and disease. How would we think about our actions in retrospect? Which ideas have led to discovery? What is the spark that is igniting the possibilities for a good future? How can we create a ripple effect that will leave the world as a better place?

    We aim to highlight individuals who have shared profound insight through their perspectives and approaches in the sciences, humanities, technologies, and arts. These individuals have made a long-lasting impact, one that enables us to feel the power of an idea worth spreading.

    • Dan Huh

      Microtech Pioneer


      Lab: BIOLines

      Fascinated by the complex structures of living human organs, Bioengineer professor and innovator Dan Huh hopes to help researchers better understand the human body and the progression of different diseases through the use of an organ-on-a-chip technology model.

      Why you should listen

      As a postdoctoral research fellow at Harvard University’s Wyss Institute, Huh worked with Donald Ingber to develop the first organs-on-chips using microengineering technologies for cell biology. These microchips served as accurate models of human organs.

      Realizing how the current models used to study disease failed to accurately simulate key biological processes that mediate disease progression and exacerbation, Huh uses the organ-on-a-chip technology to engineer predictable and cost-effective pre-clinical testing devices.

      Huh is now a Wilf Family Term Endowed Chair and an Assistant Professor in the Department of Bioengineering at the University of Pennsylvania. Huh has authored over 30 papers in Science, Nature, Nature Materials, PNAS, and other major research journals. He has won several honors and awards including the NIH Director’s New Innovator Award, which grants innovators $1.5 million during the next five years to work on groundbreaking technologies.

      What others say

      “Nature is often said to be the greatest innovator. University of Pennsylvania engineer Dan Huh, a pioneer in the development of “organs-on-chips,” tiny, three-dimensional models of living human organs, uses nature’s creativity as a source of inspiration.”
      - Madeleine Stone, Penn News 10/5/2014


    • Judy Gelles

      Artist and Storyteller


      Home: JudyGelles.com      Newsworks article      Work: Pentimenti Gallery

      Judy Gelles travelled to different communities around the world and photographed fourth graders, asking them what they wished for and what they worried about. Told in their own words, their stories touch on common human experiences and urgent social issues.

      Why you should listen

      Over the past six years, Judy Gelles interviewed and photographed over 200 fourth grade students from a wide range of economic and cultural backgrounds in China, India, South Korea, England, Saint Lucia, and multiple areas of the United States. She asked all of the students the same three questions: Who do you live with? What do you wish for? What do you worry about?

      A notable commonality across all schools was that every group of fourth-graders had very little contact with or knowledge of people from cultural or socioeconomic backgrounds that were different from their own. The Fourth Grade Project has connected children locally and globally, building bridges and tearing down walls.

      Gelles’s work can be found in major collections including the Los Angeles County Museum of Art, the Philadelphia Museum of Art, and the Museum of Modern Art in Rio de Janeiro. In 2013, Critical Mass listed her as one of the top 50 photographers in the US.

      What others say

      “Upon meeting Judy, I was met with a warm, caring smile and a genuine enthusiasm for her work. After chatting a little about the project, it was clear from the start that she possessed a real passion for art and an even bigger love of the children she was working with. Her ability to interact and work with my fourth grade class was remarkable and the students had nothing but glowing reviews of Judy.”
      - Fourth Grade Teacher, USA Jewish Day School.


    • Eric Corey Freed

      Architect


      organicARCHITECT

      Eric Corey Freed finds practical solutions for a post-carbon economy. Through integrating creativity and design, Freed has led a generation of organic and regenerative architecture that blends the natural and manmade seamlessly.

      Why you should listen

      Eric Corey Freed is calling for the adoption of a new mentality when it comes to architecture. Freed believes that today’s "harsh, style-driven fashions” in architecture can be improve through building responsibly, reducing waste, and preserving the environment. The author of Green Building & Remodeling for Dummies, Freed has sold over 200,000 copies of his book and has helped thousands of companies and dozens of municipalities around the country monetize sustainability.

      Today, Freed continues the tradition of “Organic Architecture”, first developed by Frank Lloyd Wright, and works to bridge the gap between human habitation and the natural world. A renowned leader in the sustainable architecture field, Freed was named by San Francisco Magazine as "Best Green Architect" in 2005 and "Best Visionary" in 2007. He was also named one of the "Top 10 Most Influential Green Architects."

      What others say

      “After hearing Freed’s talk, I couldn't really explain it, but I just felt better. I felt more upbeat about the future after being able to laugh about it. Perhaps in addition to trying to live green, I will try to remember to laugh my way to a green future.”
      - Bonnie Hulkower, TreeHugger Writer and Environmental Planner


    • Francis Menotti

      Theatrical Stage Magician


      Home: FrancisMenotti.com

      Fifty-two cards; the disordered mess ordered with a little sleight of hand and a lot of audience imagination. Francis ignites the hidden childlike wonder in the most unexpected place — full-grown adults.

Artfully drawing upon his education in psychology, advertising, and magic, Menotti reignites childlike wonder in every member of the audience as he explores how magic is actually meant for adults, not children.     • Jonathan Dessi-Olive

      Community Builder


      Home: JDO Architecture Design Research

      Jonathan Dessi-Olive worked with local craftsmen in Kenya to test ancient building techniques in a new context. Together, they constructed an environmentally sustainable radio studio.

      Why you should listen

      Jonathan Dessi-Olive used historical building methods to construct a hybrid wind and solar-powered radio studio on the remote Mfangano Island in Kenya. He aims to empower developing communities and build sustainable economies by adapting local construction techniques in architectural projects. In addition to the radio studio, Jonathan has contributed to a number of other projects in Kenya including a solar-powered aquaponics farm and a community center for girls.

      As the inaugural winner of the Robert A.M. Stern Traveling Fellowship, he was recognized as one of the top emerging architects who seek to perpetuate tradition through invention. He holds a Bachelor of Science in Architecture from the University of Minnesota and Master of Architecture from the University of Pennsylvania. Currently, he is a designer at Peterssen Keller Architecture in Minneapolis.

      "We were impressed by the clarity of Mr. Dessi-Olive's application of a traditional building technique to a modern problem, and its focus on sustainable architecture in the developing world."
      - Melissa DelVecchio, AIA, partner at Robert A. M. Stern Architects


    • Jack Park

      Mental Health Advocate


      PhillyinFocus

      For every one person who attempts suicide, twenty-five remain alive — Jack Park is one of the twenty-five. Jack shares his experience of living with a mental disorder and explores the ways he found love and hope after his darkest hours.

      Why you should listen

      Drawing from his personal journey to find mental wellness, Park implores us to approach our day-to-day life optimistically, practice more self-love, seek psychological help if needed, and remove the stigma attached to mental health care. He shows us the world of mental illness in a new light, so we can begin addressing issues associated with suicide, depression, and other preventable mental disorders.

      Park was featured in USA Today and Huffington Post Live about his efforts to help his peers seek support networks and overcome depression. At the University of Pennsylvania, Park wrote a viral blog post about his diagnosis and has lent a listening ear to over 100 friends. Since then, he has been featured in 19 publications and spoken at 6 events about prioritizing mental health and providing support on college campuses.

      What others say

      “[Jack’s] story has helped shift the dialogue of campus culture … with his courage to be so open about surviving the fear and pain of mental illness.”
      - USA Today


    • Jonah Berger

      Social Scientist


      Home: JonahBerger.com

      Some ideas spread like wildfire. Why do some things catch on? Jonah Berger analyzes social influence to understand why some things become popular while others fail.

      Why you should listen

      Jonah Berger combines groundbreaking research with powerful stories to illustrate why ideas catch on and what helps them spread. Having researched the principles behind social networks, word-of-mouth, and why things go viral, he helps individuals and corporations understand what drives social transmission and how to use that understanding to craft contagious content and get your own ideas to catch on.

      Berger authored New York Times bestseller Contagious: Why Things Catch On and his research is regularly covered in outlets like The New York Times, Wall Street Journal, Wired, Business Week, The Atlantic, and The Economist. He consults for companies like Google, The Gates Foundation, Facebook, General Electric, Samsung, and Coca-Cola. His research was featured in the New York Times Magazine “Year of Ideas” and most recently, Berger’s Contagious was named the Best book in Marketing by the American Marketing Association.

      What others say

      “Jonah Berger is as creative and thoughtful as he is spunky and playful. Looking at his research… provides the observer with new insights about life and also makes one aware of the creator's ingenuity and creativity. It is hard to come up with a better example of using social science to illuminate the ordinary and extraordinary in our daily lives.”
      - Dan Ariely, Behavioral Economist and TED Speaker


    • Peter Struck

      Classical Historian


      Home: Peter Struck

      Peter Struck studies ancient literature to identify links between the current idea of intuition and the ancient art of divination. With this knowledge, he aims to uncover underlying truths of our everyday action.

      Why you should listen

      Peter Struck draws from antiquity to explore intuition, a common thread that has linked humanity throughout the ages. He specializes in Roman and Greek literature and explores the history of ideas and the construction of meaning. The insights he draws from these ancient works demonstrate how our perception of the world today may not be so different from those during the height of antiquity.

      Struck is an accomplished lecturer at the University of Pennsylvania, having received the university’s highest award for teaching, the Lindback Award. At the university, he is the director of the Benjamin Franklin Scholars Program and a professor in the Classical Studies Undergraduate Department. Currently, he is writing about the applications of Greek and Roman divination in Divine Signs and Human Nature: A Cognitive History of Divination in Antiquity.

      What others say

      “Struck gives a rich and fascinating account of how the ideas of symbolism and allegorical reading developed over the course of classical antiquity. It is a remarkable achievement."
      - David Konstan, Literary Imagination

His flying robots can zip around, juggle, fly through hula-hoops, and most importantly… work together.     • Jill Wruble

      Radiologist and Ethical Provocateur


      Bio: Yale School of Medicine

      Jill Wruble addresses the problem posed by “incidentalomas,” the unexpected abnormalities that doctors discover in routine tests. She explores the medical benefits and detriments as well as the moral and financial dilemmas they pose.

      Why you should listen

      While seemingly beneficial, the discovery of incidentalomas, principally through radiology, has created major, disquieting issues. The mounting number of incidentalomas means that patients are increasingly confronted with difficult decisions whether to undergo additional tests, biopsies and surgeries or do nothing. Wruble studies the benefits of technological advances and the unnerving side effects that occur in advising patients. She aims to find a prudent perspective that promotes physical and mental well-being but is also economically sustainable.

      Wruble is a radiologist at the West Haven Connecticut Veterans Administration Medical Center. She teaches residents at Yale School of Medicine and University of Connecticut Medical Center. With her expertise in incidentalomas, she chaired a blue-ribbon panel discussion on this issue at the 2014 National Association of Veterans Affairs Physicians and Dentists Summit.

      What others say

      "Logical and sharp, [Jill’s] concepts are a shining example of critical thinking. [Her] unusually organized and methodical approach to reading studies and verbalizing [her] thought processes are the true asset in science and medicine.”
      - Ali Mian, M.D., Yale University School of Medicine, President, Resident and Fellow Senate


    • Lyle Ungar

      Psychologist and Computer Scientist


      Home: Lyle Ungar      Article: Psychological Science

      Can Twitter predict heart disease? Lyle Ungar shows how Twitter can capture more information about heart disease than 10 traditional demographic, socioeconomic, and health risk factors.

      Why you should listen

      Lyle Ungar analyzed 148 million tweets from more than 1,300 counties that represent 88 percent of the U.S. population. He uncovered that language and mortality have a strong relationship, even after variables like education and income were accounted for. Positive emotional language like “wonderful” in a community seems to offer protection against heart disease, while swearing correlates with increased mortality. Ungar aims to use this understanding to foster optimism in communities and make people happier and healthier.

      Ungar is a Professor of Computer and Information Science at the University of Pennsylvania. He has published over 200 articles and holds eleven patents. His current research focuses on developing scalable machine learning methods for data and text mining, including analysis of social media to better understand what determines physical and mental well-being.

      What others say

      "Twitter seems to capture a lot of the same information that you get from health and demographic indicators, but it also adds something extra. Predictions from Twitter can actually be more accurate than using a set of traditional variables."
      - Gregory Park, University of Pennsylvania Department of Psychology

      Peacebuilder


      Bio: PeaceTech Lab

      For the first time in history, anybody can send information, photos, and money around the world with the click of a button. Sheldon Himelfarb empowers conflict-ridden communities with “PeaceTech” tools to mitigate causes of violence.

      Why you should listen

      Sheldon Himelfarb seeks to revolutionize the way people in conflict-ridden areas use technology to resolve international conflicts. The newly dubbed “PeaceTech industry” has the potential to reduce election violence, interethnic hatred, land disputes, and gender violence. In an effort to develop people-to-people diplomacy, Himelfarb has worked to bring security and stability to communities around the world from Iraq to Bosnia to Afghanistan.

      Himelfarb is the CEO and President of the PeaceTech Lab at the United States Institute of Peace and has led efforts in peacebuilding, foreign policy, and conflict management. Prior to this, he served as a foreign policy adviser to a member of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee. He is an award-winning filmmaker, former commentator for National Public Radio, and author of numerous articles on politics, popular culture and media.

      What others say

      "Sheldon Himelfarb is leading a new paradigm through the PeaceTech Lab: making the world a better place for all by bringing the peacebuilding community and the Science and Technology community together ... preventing violent conflicts, empowering the youth, enticing innovation and promoting entrepreneurship.     • Spencer Penn

      Social Entrepreneur


      Article: NYTimes      Home: Sweet Bites Gum

      Spencer Penn is the co-founder of a social venture created to fight cavities in developing countries. Tooth decay is the world’s most common chronic illness affecting roughly 3.9 billion people or 55% of the world population. His team’s solution? Cavity-fighting Chewing Gum sweetened with Xylitol.

      Why you should listen

      Candy saves lives. Spencer Penn and Penn students Thoba Grenville-Grey, Eric Kauderer-Abrams, Morgan Snyder, and Joshua Tycko co-founded Sweet Bites - a social venture that manufactures cavity fighting chewing gum. The single-serving packaging, printed with health messages, is distributed through traditional corner stores and school-lunch programs. With the help of local female dental students, NGO's, Penn Dental, and the Clinton Global Initiative, Sweet Bites has piloted projects with their Xylitol gum in 5 continents.

      Sweet Bites was selected as a global finalist for the prestigious Hult Prize and its co-founders were invited to speak at the the annual Clinton Global Initiative conference in New York. In 2014, Spencer Penn graduated from the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania and is currently a Robotics Engineering Masters student at the university. Spencer served as the 2014 Class President at Penn for four years in undergrad.

      What others say

      “The beauty of what they’ve put in place is if they have to walk away, and it doesn’t work out, they will have done good.”
      - Ian MacMillan, professor at the University of Pennsylvania


    • David Gruber

      Marine Biologist


      Bio: National Geographic Explorers      Website: Luminescent Lab

      David Gruber explores the undersea world - and it glows. He will show you this colorful world of the unseen on a deep-sea dive.

      Why you should listen

      David Gruber uncovered the secret language of biofluorescence in marine animals, such as swell sharks and false moray eels. He studies fluorescent proteins and how marine creatures use colors to communicate, interact, and avoid enemies. With insights into the fluorescent world that humans cannot detect, Gruber and his collaborators have found a novel way for us to perceive and better understand life in the sea.

      Committed to ocean conservation and the public understanding of science, Gruber is an Emerging Explorer at National Geographic. His research has been featured in exhibits at American Museum of Natural History. Besides regularly diving into the ocean, he is an Associate Professor of Biology at the City University of New York and a Research Associate at the American Museum of Natural History.

      What others say

      “Gruber conveys the spirit of scientific endeavor perfectly: discovery is not just a goal, but an unpredictable process as dependent on intellectual genius as on creative inspiration and pure luck … biofluorescence is revolutionary: use of fluorescent protein technology is now the order of the day”
      - Jennifer Lippincott-Schwartz, National Institute of Health
